# The Last Tuesday

In a small city near Tirunelveli Junction, a man named Vivin ran a lathe shop. He was good at his work, and known for being honest. One day, a poor man came to his shop looking for a job. Vivin hired him on the spot and paid him fairly.

For years, Vivin and his assistant kept the lathe running. Orders piled up, so Vivin took the night shift himself and let his assistant work mornings. Between the two of them, the shop never slept.

After his shifts, Vivin went home to his wife and son, and the two of them raised the boy like a little king. Vivin was well liked in the neighborhood — the kind of man people nodded to on the street.

In his late forties, he noticed a boy in the area who worked hard and worked smart, and something about the boy's attitude stuck with him. They started meeting at a tea shop at midnight — both of them night owls by habit — and Vivin always paid for the coffee. They'd talk business, politics, the state of the world, the small stuff that fills up a friendship.

Then one Tuesday, while he was deep in work, Vivin felt suddenly uneasy. In a snap, he was somewhere else — a beach, standing next to a child, both of them confused. Before he could make sense of it, he was back in his shop.

He thought about it all day. The feeling faded over the week, but he was afraid to tell anyone — afraid of being judged, afraid he was losing his mind. He stopped sleeping deeply. Four hours a night, no more.

Another Tuesday came. He went to work as usual, and while he was bent over the lathe, a strange coin-sized portal opened in front of him. Curious, he touched it — and the confusion took him again. The same beach. This time, a teenage boy stood there, and neither of them felt like strangers meeting for the first time.

"How long have you been here?" Vivin asked.

"I've been here long before you," the boy said. "I've been looking for you for years. And — finally — here you are again."

"How do we get back to your place?"

"My place? What do you mean, my place?"

Vivin looked down at the boy's hand. "Why don't your fingers have prints?"

The boy raised an eyebrow. "Look at your own hand."

Vivin looked. No prints there either. Something in his chest went cold, but he didn't say anything.

They walked to the far end of the beach and found a bar — a strange one, where everyone who arrived was handed their favorite drink without a word exchanged. Curious, Vivin waited his turn. The bartender set down an Appy Fizz in a beer glass. Vivin stared at it, stunned.

The boy laughed. "I thought your favorite had changed."

Vivin asked him the time.

"The — what?" the boy said, like the words were unfamiliar. Vivin repeated the question, and the boy just shook his head. "New word to me." That was when Vivin noticed there was no sunrise here, and none coming. No time at all.

He left the boy and went to explore. Further down, he found his dream car parked outside a café and ran his hand along it like an old friend. A man stepped out of the café, saw him, and said, "Let's go for a ride." Vivin was happier than he'd been in years.

They drove for a long while. The man talked about the place — what it was, what it wasn't — and it turned into an easy, familiar conversation, the kind you have with someone you've known forever. From the rearview mirror hung a small keychain, shaped like a tiny lathe chisel, its edges worn smooth from years of handling. Vivin knew it without asking — the one his assistant had cast for him the year the shop turned twenty. He said nothing. Time passed. A long time. Vivin grew old in that stretch of road, though he couldn't say how. And then he slipped, fell — and opened his eyes back in his own world.

He nearly cried with relief. He ran home like a boy, waving at the baby on the corner the way he did every morning. The street dogs came bounding toward him — but ran past, tails wagging, toward the gate behind him. He didn't stop to wonder why.

He slipped in through the back gate, into the hall — and stopped.

Three figures stood waiting in the half-light. A boy, holding a bottle of Appy Fizz. A teenager, still listening for a sound that never came. And a man, turning a small lathe-shaped keychain over and over between his fingers.

Himself. All of himself, at every age he had ever been, standing in his own house, looking back at him.

His eyes went red before his mind caught up with why. The tears came first — some part of him had already understood, long before the rest of him did. He hadn't been visiting them, all those Tuesdays. He had never been the one arriving. He was one of them, another stage, waiting his turn to stand in a hallway like this and watch someone else walk in.

He'd had a mild heart attack the Tuesday before.

This Tuesday, the second one, had killed him.
